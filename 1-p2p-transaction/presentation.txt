P2P MONEY TRANSFER SYSTEM — ARCHITECTURE REVIEW
Presentation Transcript
================================================================================

Audience: Engineering team
Length: ~30 slides
Estimated speaking time: 35–45 minutes

SLIDE 1 — TITLE: P2P Money Transfer System

Good morning, everyone. Today I'm walking you through the system architecture
for a real-time peer-to-peer money transfer service — think Zelle or Venmo. The
five numbers at the bottom of this slide define the problem space we're
designing into: fifty million registered users, five million daily actives,
two hundred transactions per second on average bursting to a thousand, four
nines of availability, and a p99 latency budget of three seconds end to end.

The two design ideas I want you to leave with today are: money lives in
DynamoDB, not Postgres; and money never actually moves until settlement —
the synchronous path only places a hold.


SLIDE 2 — Problem statement & agenda


The problem in one sentence: design a real-time P2P money transfer system,
identified by phone or email, US dollars only, with instant perceived
settlement.

The core constraints in the bottom-left card are not negotiable. Money must
never be lost or created. Balances must be consistent under concurrent
updates. Retries must never cause a double transfer. And every state change
must be auditable for seven years.

We'll move through seven sections — requirements and scale, then the six
design principles, then the high-level architecture, then the data model and
money flow, then the transfer flows themselves, then trade-offs, and finally
the deep-dive Q&A that an interviewer or a senior engineer would push us on.


SLIDE 3 — Functional scope


Six cards on this slide. Core features are send, receive, balance, history.
Identification is by phone or email — users never enter a bank account
number. Currency is USD only — no FX in scope. Limits are five hundred
dollars per transaction and twenty-five hundred per day. Settlement is
"instant" from the user's perspective, not next-day ACH. And explicitly out
of scope: account creation, investments, and card payments. That last bullet
matters because it's what takes PCI-DSS off our compliance plate.


SLIDE 4 — Non-functional targets


These six numbers drive almost every architectural decision later in the
deck. Fifty million users at five million DAU is medium-large scale. Two
hundred TPS average isn't extreme, but peak of a thousand and a future tenx
target shapes our partitioning strategy. The three-second p99 means the
synchronous path has to stay short — which is exactly why fraud, AML and
settlement get pushed off into an async workflow. Four nines means we have
about fifty minutes of downtime per year — call it four-and-a-half minutes
per month — so we need multi-AZ everywhere and graceful degradation paths.
And seven-year retention is the regulatory anchor for the ledger sizing.


SLIDE 5 — Security & compliance scope


Four bands. KYC and authentication are handled upstream — we assume users
arrive with a verified JWT, and we verify it at the gateway. Fraud is split:
cheap deterministic rules run inline, and ML scoring is an external API we
only call synchronously when we already think a transaction is high-risk.
AML screening is an external API too — sanctions-list matching, not built
in-house. SOC2 is our compliance target. PCI-DSS is out of scope because
we never handle card data — we operate on internal balances.


SLIDE 6 — Back-of-the-envelope capacity


This is the slide that drives our storage choice. Two hundred TPS times two
ledger rows per transfer — one debit, one credit — times the seconds in a
year gives us roughly twelve-point-six billion ledger rows per year. Over
seven years of retention that's about eighty-eight billion rows. At one
kilobyte per row we're looking at thirteen terabytes per year, ninety
terabytes total.

Two important takeaways. First, reads outnumber writes about tenx — balance
checks and history queries. Second, on the right: all amounts are bigint
minor units, cents. Never floating point. We keep a currency code on every
row so we can extend to multi-currency later, but the arithmetic itself is
strictly integer.


SLIDE 7 — Six design principles


These six principles are the lens for every decision later. Correctness
first — money is neither lost nor created. Idempotency — client-generated
keys make retries safe at every layer. Immutable audit log — the ledger is
append-only, and balance is the aggregated result, never overwritten.
Tiered consistency — money is strongly consistent, notifications and view
caches are eventually consistent. Failure-oriented design — assume every
component fails, eliminate single points of failure. And finally, the one
that's most architecturally distinctive: sync hold, async settle. We'll
unpack that one in detail on slide eighteen.


SLIDE 8 — Polyglot persistence: DynamoDB for money


This is the headline storage decision. Twelve-point-six billion rows per
year is more than we want to run on PostgreSQL. So money — the balance
items and the full double-entry ledger — lives in DynamoDB. PostgreSQL
keeps everything else: users, identifiers, transfer metadata, workflow
state, and a display cache for balance.

Two things I want to call out from the right column. The display cache is
refreshed asynchronously from DynamoDB Streams. It is never authoritative.
It is never used for the debit decision. If staleness in the cache could
cause a wrong debit, we'd have a bug. The decision to debit is always made
against DynamoDB.


SLIDE 9 — High-level component view


Top to bottom. At the edge, requests pass through CloudFront, then WAF,
then API Gateway or ALB which terminates TLS, verifies the JWT, and routes.

In the services tier on the left, the big teal box is the Payments Service.
This is the money-movement core — transfer flow, idempotency control, limit
checks, balance and ledger writes, history reads. It owns the writes to the
balance. Nothing else writes balance.

On the right, three independent services: User Directory for resolving
phone or email to a user ID, Fraud Rule Engine for risk checks, and
Notification for push, SMS, and email — all asynchronous.

At the bottom, the data tier: DynamoDB for the authoritative money,
Aurora PostgreSQL for metadata and the display cache, ElastiCache for rate
limiting and sessions, and Kafka via MSK for our async event bus and
transactional outbox.


SLIDE 10 — Service decomposition: by data, not by URL


This is the principle behind that diagram. We do not split services along
URL paths. We split along transactional and data-ownership boundaries.

On the left: the transfer flow, the balance and ledger writes, and the
transaction history are all inside the same Payments Service because they
read and write the same data and have to participate in the same ACID
transaction. Splitting them by URL — one service for slash-transfers,
another for slash-accounts — would force a distributed transaction or a
Saga on the critical money-movement path. That's accidental complexity for
no benefit.

On the right: Fraud, User Directory, and Notification are genuinely
independent. Different data, different failure modes, different scaling
profiles. Their failures should never block money movement.


SLIDE 11 — Why this split — vs monolith, vs URL-based


Quick comparison table. A pure monolith gets money atomicity easily but
couples notification failures to the money core. A URL-based split gets
fault isolation but pays for it with distributed transactions on the
critical path. Our split keeps the atomic core whole — Payments owns both
the DynamoDB writes and the Postgres metadata — while peeling off the
genuinely independent concerns.

The italicized callout at the bottom is the incremental-design principle:
start with these four services, split further only when a real
transactional or scaling boundary demands it. Never because a different
URL prefix exists.


SLIDE 12 — Key components & responsibilities


Six rows. The API Gateway terminates TLS, verifies JWTs, rate-limits, and
routes. The Payments Service is the money-movement core — it's the sole
owner of balance updates. User Directory resolves email and phone to user
IDs. The Fraud Rule Engine runs inline rule checks AND it's the sole
caller of the external ML and AML APIs — we'll come back to that on the
next slide. The Notification Service does push, SMS, email — all async.
And the Event Bus, Kafka or SQS, carries async inter-service messages and
the transactional outbox.


SLIDE 13 — External integrations: encapsulated by Fraud Service


This is a small but important architectural decision. The Payments Service
does NOT call external risk APIs directly. It calls the Fraud Service, and
the Fraud Service is the sole integration point for ML scoring and AML
screening.

Why? Because vendor SDKs, credentials, fallback policies, and false-positive
handling are operationally messy. Keeping them inside a dedicated service
means none of that complexity bleeds into the money-movement core.

The bank network — RTP, FedNow — is externalized too. We treat it as a
stable downstream dependency, protected by circuit breaker, timeout, and
fallback.


SLIDE 14 — Kubernetes on EKS, multi-AZ


Six rows on deployment. We're on Amazon EKS, multi-AZ. Autoscaling uses
HPA based on CPU and a custom TPS metric, plus the Cluster Autoscaler for
nodes. Service mesh — Istio or App Mesh — gives us mTLS, retries, circuit
breaking, and observability. Secrets come from AWS Secrets Manager via the
External Secrets Operator. Deployments are rolling or canary via Argo
Rollouts — zero downtime is non-negotiable at four nines. And pod placement
uses PodAntiAffinity and topology spread constraints so no service is ever
concentrated in a single AZ.

The data stores stay outside the cluster as managed services. Smaller blast
radius, less operational overhead.


SLIDE 15 — Cross-cutting concerns


Eight tiles you'd expect on any production system. Auth, metrics, tracing,
logging, alerting, audit, config management, rate limiting. The one I want
to flag specifically is the audit log — append-only with S3 Object Lock
in WORM mode, seven-year retention. That's a SOC2 hard requirement and it
also gives us a tamper-resistant story for regulator audits.


SLIDE 16 — SLIs and SLOs


Four headline numbers. Transfer success rate at or above ninety-nine
point ninety-five percent. p99 latency at or below three seconds.
Availability at four nines, which is about fifty minutes per year. And
balance consistency — one hundred percent, verified by daily reconciliation.

The bottom panel is how we actually verify these. The most important is
continuous reconciliation: we replay each account's settled ledger and
check that the chain holds — previous plus amount equals next equals
current available. And we verify that each account's "held" counter
matches the sum of its in-flight transfers. Any divergence raises an alert
because at that point we have a real correctness problem.


SLIDE 17 — Data model: entities & ownership


Eight entities, color-coded by store. Two in DynamoDB — balance and
ledger. Six in PostgreSQL — users, identifiers, account metadata,
transfer metadata, workflow events, idempotency keys.

The teal callout at the bottom is the core invariant: spendable equals
available minus held. And every balance mutation conditions on the
version field and bumps it. That's the optimistic lock that prevents
lost updates — we'll see it in action on slide twenty-seven.


SLIDE 18 — Hold-then-settle, no escrow


This is the most distinctive piece of the design. Three columns, one per
phase.

At POST, synchronous: we reserve. One conditional UpdateItem on the
sender's balance. Held increases by the amount, version bumps. The
condition is that available minus held is still greater than or equal to
the amount — that's the overspend check, atomic with the write. No
ledger entry. No money has moved.

At settlement, asynchronous, after fraud review and the moratorium: we
move. One TransactWriteItems. Sender's available drops by the amount,
held drops by the amount, recipient's available rises by the amount, and
we append both ledger entries. The transaction is conditioned on the
idempotency key not having settled before — exactly-once settlement.

On cancel or deny: we release the hold. Held drops by the amount. No
ledger entry, because no money ever moved. There's nothing to claw back.

That last point is the elegance. Cancellation is just letting go of a
hold. It's not a compensating transaction. There's no escrow account
sitting in the middle. Money moves exactly once, at settlement.


SLIDE 19 — API contract


Six endpoints. POST slash-transfers creates and executes a transfer — and
the Idempotency-Key header is required. GETs are naturally idempotent.
POST slash-transfers-slash-ID-slash-cancel cancels during the moratorium
window, before settlement. POST slash-transfers-slash-ID-slash-reverse is
the post-settlement refund — that's a compensating transaction, and it
also requires an idempotency key.

Every write carries Authorization Bearer JWT and an Idempotency-Key
header generated by the client.


SLIDE 20 — Synchronous transfer flow


Five steps in the sync path, and the goal is to return within the p99
latency budget.

Step one: insert the transfer metadata row in PostgreSQL with the
idempotency key. Unique constraint catches replays — a duplicate just
returns the stored response.

Step two: limit check. Five hundred per transaction, twenty-five hundred
per day. Cheap, deterministic, reject early.

Step three: resolve the recipient identifier — phone or email — to a
user ID via the User Directory service.

Step four: reserve funds. The conditional UpdateItem we just talked about.

Step five: return 200 OK with status DEBITED, and publish a
TransferRequested event to Kafka.

That's it for the sync path. Everything else is async.


SLIDE 21 — Async settlement workflow


Four stages, and the critical property is that this workflow is durable
and resumable. State lives in the transfers status field and the
transfer_events table — both in PostgreSQL. A crash mid-workflow just
re-drives from the last persisted state. If this gets complex enough, we
move it to Step Functions which gives us durable execution out of the
box.

Stage one: fraud and AML. Most transfers pass cleanly. A small fraction
go to human approval.

Stage two: moratorium. We wait until the settle_after timestamp. This
gives the user a cancellation window. It's risk-tiered — for low-risk
transfers we set it to near zero, so settlement is effectively instant.
For high-risk we apply a longer hold.

Stage three: settlement. The TransactWriteItems we keep referring to.
Money actually moves here, exactly once.

Stage four: notifications fan out.

And if at stage one or two the answer is deny or cancel — release the
hold. No ledger entry. Money never moved.


SLIDE 22 — Security: defense in depth


Eight tiles. Standard layered security. The bits I'll highlight are
specific to our domain: amount bounds and positive-value checks at input
validation — never trust the client to send a sane number. Per-user and
per-IP rate limiting with token buckets. The fraud bullet — ML and AML
only via the Fraud Service, never directly from Payments. And the audit
log with WORM via S3 Object Lock, immutable for seven years.


SLIDE 23 — Trade-off: DynamoDB for money, PostgreSQL for metadata


The detailed trade-off table for the polyglot persistence choice. Five
rows. DynamoDB holds the balance items and the full ledger; PostgreSQL
holds users, transfer metadata, workflow events, and the display cache.
DynamoDB gives us TransactWriteItems for atomic balanced entries;
PostgreSQL gives us multi-row ACID for metadata. DynamoDB scales near-
unlimited horizontally by account_id; PostgreSQL is bounded and easy to
operate. DynamoDB is strongly consistent on the conditional writes; the
cache is eventually consistent through DynamoDB Streams.

The trade-off callout at the bottom is the honest one: putting money in
DynamoDB means overspend prevention and ordering have to be expressed as
conditional appends rather than SQL SERIALIZABLE. We accepted that
constraint because the ledger volume is operationally infeasible in
PostgreSQL.


SLIDE 24 — Other tunable decisions


Eight more axes where the design could go differently and what the trade-
off would be. The check marks show the choice we made.

Transfer confirmation — fully sync is simpler UX but no room for fraud
review or cancellation. We chose sync hold plus async settle.

Moratorium length — tunable per risk tier, zero for low-risk effectively
gives instant delivery.

Consistency model — money strong, display and notifications eventual.

Hot accounts — single ledger by default; if one account becomes a real
hotspot, model it as sub-accounts.

Messaging, fraud, multi-region, payment network — each row has a sensible
default and a forward-looking expansion path.


SLIDE 25 — Deep dive Q1: crash recovery


Now we're into the questions a senior engineer or interviewer will push
us on.

Question one: if the server crashes mid-transfer, how do you recover?
The answer is at the top: no money moves until settlement, which is a
single atomic DynamoDB TransactWriteItems. A crash before the commit
leaves no partial debit or credit — it's all-or-nothing.

Four points below. The sync step only places a hold — a crash there
leaves at most an orphaned hold which is harmless and gets swept by
reconciliation. Workflow state is durable in PostgreSQL, not in-memory,
so we re-drive from the last persisted state. Settlement is idempotent —
conditioned on the idempotency key not yet settled, so retries are no-ops.
And if orchestration gets complex, Step Functions gives us durable
execution for free.


SLIDE 26 — Deep dive Q2: idempotency


Question two: how do you prevent double transfers on retries?

Two layers. Layer one in PostgreSQL: the transfers table has a unique
constraint on the idempotency key. A duplicate insert conflicts, and we
return the stored response. We also store the request hash so we can
reject the case where the same key arrives with a different body —
that's a client bug we should surface, not absorb.

Layer two — and this is the one that matters most — in DynamoDB. The
TransactWriteItems for settlement includes a guard item keyed by the
transfer ID, with an attribute_not_exists condition. So even if the same
transaction is retried, the conditional write fails and the balance is
debited exactly once. Idempotency is protected at the money write
itself, not only at the metadata layer.


SLIDE 27 — Deep dive Q3: concurrency


Question three: how do you ensure consistency under concurrent updates?

The answer is the optimistic lock on the balance's version field. Every
write reads version equals V, conditions on version equals V, and sets
version to V plus one.

Three scenarios on this slide. Two settlements crediting the same
recipient — only one commits, the other aborts on the version mismatch
and retries. A debit and a credit racing on the same account — the
version chain forces an order, both succeed exactly once, and the ledger
records the actual sequence. And an overspend attempt — the available
minus held check is atomic with the conditional write, so there's no
TOCTOU window, no overspend.

The decision is never made against the PostgreSQL cache. Debits always
read and condition on DynamoDB.


SLIDE 28 — Deep dive Q4: SPOFs


Question four: where are the single points of failure?

Going through the layers. Money store — DynamoDB is multi-AZ and
regionally replicated by design, so no single node to lose. Metadata —
Aurora multi-AZ with read replicas and automatic failover. Cache —
ElastiCache cluster mode; and remember the Postgres balance cache is
non-authoritative, so even if it goes stale a debit isn't affected.
Messaging — MSK across multiple brokers. Compute — EKS multi-AZ. Edge —
DNS failover via CloudFront and Route 53. External APIs — circuit
breaker plus timeout plus fallback; if ML scoring is down, we degrade
to rule-based.


SLIDE 29 — Deep dive Q5: 10x scale


Question five: at ten thousand TPS, where's the bottleneck?

The headline is that DynamoDB scales near-linearly because it partitions
by account_id. The money store is not our first wall.

The first real risk is a single very hot account — like a massive
merchant. Per-account writes serialize on the seq chain, which is
correct but is a hotspot. The fix is to model that one account as N
sub-accounts and route and aggregate across them. We only apply this to
the rare hotspot.

Keep the sync path minimal — one hold UpdateItem, nothing else. Use
DynamoDB on-demand or auto-scaling. PostgreSQL is bounded relative to
the ledger — scale with read replicas, partition by account_id only if
needed, absorb connection count with RDS Proxy. Messaging scales by
adding Kafka partitions. Workers scale as stateless replicas.


SLIDE 30 — Key takeaways: what this design gets right


Six takeaways to close.

One — polyglot persistence: DynamoDB for money, PostgreSQL for metadata,
driven by the twelve-point-six billion rows per year scale number.

Two — hold-then-settle without escrow: sync hold for instant UX, async
settlement to allow fraud review and cancellation.

Three — idempotency at the money write itself: two layers, PostgreSQL
unique key plus DynamoDB conditional guard.

Four — optimistic locking on balance.version: serializes concurrent
updates to the same account, no lost updates.

Five — boundaries follow data, not URLs: money core stays atomic, risk
and notification stay independent.

Six — immutable append-only ledger: double-entry, seven-year retention,
WORM audit, reconciled daily.

Closing line: correctness first, idempotent everywhere, no SPOFs,
reconcile continuously. Happy to take questions.
